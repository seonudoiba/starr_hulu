# UNIT 1: UNDERSTANDING THE GAME

## 1.1 What Band 9 Actually Means

Most Nigerians think Band 9 means "perfect English like a British professor." **That is wrong.**

Band 9 means: *You have a fully operational command of the language. Your use of English is appropriate, accurate, and fluent, with complete understanding.*

**Key words:**
- **Appropriate** (not fancy – you say "children" not "offspring" unless it fits)
- **Accurate** (very few errors – maybe 1 or 2 in a full essay)
- **Fluent** (no unnatural pauses in speaking)
- **Complete understanding** (you never misunderstand a question or text)

**The official Band 9 descriptors simplified:**

| Skill | What Band 9 requires (simplified) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Listening | Get 39–40 out of 40 correct. Miss 1 maximum. |
| Reading | Get 39–40 out of 40 correct. Miss 1 maximum. |
| Writing | Task achievement: fully answers all parts. Cohesion: flows perfectly. Vocabulary: precise, natural, flexible. Grammar: 1–2 minor errors at most. |
| Speaking | Flows like a native. Any accent is fine as long as it's clear. Vocabulary is precise. Grammar is error-free almost always. |

**The truth:** Many native speakers (including Nigerians who grew up speaking English at home) score Band 7.5–8.0 on IELTS. Band 9 requires **training**, not just fluency.

## 1.2 Why Nigerians Often Get Stuck at 6.5–7.0

This section is critical. Read it twice.

Based on real IELTS results from Nigerian test-takers (British Council data and examiner reports), these are the **five biggest barriers** for Nigerians:

#### Barrier #1: Article Errors (a/an/the)
As mentioned in the sample chapter. Yoruba and Igbo have no articles. Hausa has `da` and `din` but they don't work like English.
- **Result:** You lose 0.5–1.0 bands in Writing. At Band 9, this is fatal.

#### Barrier #2: Preposition Errors
Nigerians frequently say:
- "Discuss **about** the problem" (wrong – "discuss the problem")
- "Emphasis **on**" is correct, but "discuss about" is a direct translation from Nigerian English usage.
- "He graduated **from** the university" (some Nigerians say "graduated **off**" or "graduated **at**")

#### Barrier #3: "Flowery" English
In Nigerian secondary schools, students are rewarded for using big words. IELTS punishes this if it feels unnatural.
- **Band 6 essay:** *"It is indubitably evident that the phenomenon of unemployment is a pernicious and deleterious predicament..."*
- **Band 9 essay:** *"Unemployment is a serious problem because it affects millions of families directly."*

The Band 9 sentence is **simpler** and **clearer**. IELTS examiners are not impressed by big words. They are impressed by precise words.

#### Barrier #4: Pronunciation of 'th' and Vowel Length
- **'th' sounds:** Many Nigerians pronounce 'three' as 'tree', 'think' as 'tink', 'that' as 'dat', 'mother' as 'mover'.  
  - At Band 9, this must be fixed. The examiner must never misunderstand you.
- **Vowel length:** 'Sheep' vs 'ship', 'beach' vs 'bitch', 'full' vs 'fool'. These are different words in English. Mixing them changes meaning.

#### Barrier #5: Writing Task 2 – Not Answering the Question
The biggest killer. The question asks: *"Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?"*  
The Nigerian candidate writes only the advantages. Or describes the problem without giving an opinion.  
- **Result:** Task Response score = 5.0. Overall Writing score cannot go above 6.0.

**Self-test:** Which of these five barriers affect you?  
Tick the box in your notebook. You will return to these in every unit.

## 1.3 How IELTS Is Different from WAEC/NECO English

This is where many Nigerians go wrong. They prepare for IELTS like they prepared for WAEC. **That is a mistake.**

| Feature | WAEC/NECO English | IELTS (Academic) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Essay style | Long, descriptive, flowery, often narrative | Short, structured, argumentative, factual |
| Time pressure | 45–60 minutes per essay | 40 minutes for Writing Task 2 + 20 for Task 1 |
| Grammar marking | Punishes some errors but rewards vocabulary | Punishes ALL errors. Vocabulary must be precise, not big. |
| Listening | Clear Nigerian/British accent | Multiple accents (British, Australian, Canadian, American) |
| Speaking | Not always tested | Face-to-face with an examiner. Critical. |
| Reading | Comprehension questions in order | Questions are scrambled. Time management is everything. |

**The biggest difference:** WAEC rewards how much you know. IELTS rewards how **clearly** and **accurately** you communicate. A simple, error-free sentence beats a complex, error-filled sentence every time.

# UNIT 2: LISTENING FOR PERFECTION

## 2.1 Why Nigerians Lose Points in Listening (Even When They Understand Everything)

You might think: "I understand English perfectly. Why do I miss answers?"

Here is the truth based on examiner reports: **Nigerians lose more points on spelling and numbers than on understanding.**

The most common mistakes:

| Mistake type | Example (wrong → right) | How often it happens |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Spelling errors** | "accomodation" → "accommodation" | Very frequent |
| **Plural 's' missing** | "student" → "students" (when the audio says plural) | Very frequent |
| **Numbers: 15 vs 50** | Writing "15" when audio says "fifty" | Frequent |
| **Dates** | Writing "Jan 5" → "5 January" (format matters) | Frequent |
| **Name spelling** | "Brain" → "Brian" (common Nigerian mishearing) | Frequent |
| **Homophones** | "their/there/they're" – writing the wrong one | Occasional |

**The fix:** In IELTS Listening, spelling, plurals, and numbers are **not** marked for meaning. If the answer is "accommodation" and you write "accomodation", it is **wrong** – even if the meaning is clear.

**Band 9 requirement:** You must get 39 or 40 correct. That means at most 1 spelling or number error across the whole test.

---

## 2.2 The Four Sections of IELTS Listening (Know the Pattern)

| Section | Topic | Number of questions | Accents you will hear |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Social survival (booking a hotel, joining a library) – usually a phone call | 10 | British (often regional: Manchester, London) |
| **2** | Social monologue (a tour guide speaking, a radio announcement) | 10 | British, Australian, or New Zealand |
| **3** | Educational/training conversation (2–4 students or a student & tutor discussing an assignment) | 10 | Mix: British, North American, sometimes Indian or other |
| **4** | Academic monologue (a university lecture) | 10 | Usually British or Australian – fastest speech |

**Key strategy for Nigerians:**
- **Sections 1 and 2** are easier. You should get 19–20/20 here.
- **Section 3** can be tricky because of multiple speakers overlapping or interrupting. Practice with group conversations.
- **Section 4** is the hardest. You have to follow a lecture for 5+ minutes and write answers while listening. This requires intense concentration.

---

## 2.3 The #1 Skill: Predicting Before You Listen

Most Nigerians listen first and then try to answer. **That is backwards.**

At Band 9, you must predict what kind of answer you need before the audio starts.

**How to predict (in the 30 seconds before each section):**

1. Read the questions quickly.
2. For each blank or multiple-choice option, ask yourself:
   - Is it a **number**? (date, time, price, age, quantity)
   - Is it a **name**? (person, place, company – listen for capitalisation clues)
   - Is it a **noun**? (object, thing)
   - Is it an **adjective**? (colour, size, opinion)
   - Is it a **verb**? (action – often in -ing form)

3. Write a tiny symbol next to the blank to remind you.
   - `#` = number
   - `N` = name
   - `adj` = adjective
   - `v` = verb

**Example from real IELTS:**

*The tour will visit the ______ museum.*

Your prediction: The blank is likely a **name** (e.g., "British Museum") or an **adjective** (e.g., "modern"). You listen for either.

*The cost per person is $______ .*

Prediction: **number** (#). Listen for a price. Could be "fifteen" or "fifty" – be ready.

**Exercise:** Predict the answer type for these 5 blanks (answers at end of unit):

1. *The meeting will be held in room ______ .*
2. *Students should bring their own ______ .*
3. *The lecture starts at ______ .*
4. *Professor Williams recommends reading ______ .*
5. *The company was founded in ______ .*

## 2.4 Spelling: The Nigerian Killer (With Specific Fixes)

Spelling is not about intelligence. It is about **visual memory** – your brain's ability to remember what a word looks like.

**The most commonly misspelled words by Nigerians in IELTS Listening:**

| Correct spelling | Common Nigerian error | Memory trick |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| accommodation | accomodation | "two c's, two m's" – think "two beds" |
| government | goverment | contains "govern" + "ment" |
| environment | enviroment | contains "environ" (like "around") |
| separate | seperate | "there is 'a rat' in separate" |
| necessary | neccessary | one 'c', two 's's – "one collar, two socks" |
| February | Febuary | remember the first 'r' (say "Feb-RU-ary") |
| Wednesday | Wenesday | "Wed-NES-day" |
| definitely | definately | "finite" in the middle |
| privilege | privelege | "privi" + "lege" – no 'd' |
| recommend | recomend | one 'c', two 'm's |

**How to train spelling for Band 9:**
- **Day 1-7:** Write each of these 10 words 10 times (100 total writings).
- **Day 8-14:** Have someone dictate the words to you. Write them. Check.
- **Day 15 onwards:** Whenever you misspell a word in a practice test, add it to your "Naija Spelling Jail" list. Write it 20 times.

**Plurals:** If the audio says "books", you must write "books". Not "book". IELTS Listening is ruthless on missing 's'.

**Tip for plurals:** When you predict, note whether the blank is likely to be plural. Look for clues like "some", "many", "a few", or verbs that agree with plural subjects.

## 2.5 Numbers: 15 vs 50, 13 vs 30, Dates, Times

Nigerians frequently confuse:
- **15 (fifteen)** vs **50 (fifty)** – the stress is different.
  - "Fifteen" has stress on the **second** syllable: fif-TEEN (longer 'ee')
  - "Fifty" has stress on the **first** syllable: FIF-ty (short 'i')
- **13 (thirteen)** vs **30 (thirty)** – same pattern.
- **18 (eighteen)** vs **80 (eighty)**.

**How to hear the difference:** The audio will say "fifteen" with a clear 'teen' sound that is longer. "Fifty" sounds shorter and ends with a 'ty' sound.

**Practice exercise (say these aloud or listen to YouTube pronunciation videos):**
- 14 vs 40
- 16 vs 60
- 17 vs 70
- 19 vs 90

**Dates:**
- IELTS uses both British (day/month/year) and American (month/day/year) formats. But the listening will say it clearly.
- If you hear "the fifth of January", write **5 January** or **January 5** – both are accepted. But be consistent.
- Do NOT write "5th January" – the 'th' is not needed and might be marked wrong if the answer key expects "5 January".

**Times:**
- "half past two" = 2:30 (write 2.30 or 2:30 – both are fine)
- "a quarter to three" = 2:45
- "ten to nine" = 8:50
- You can write "8.50am" or "8:50 am" – but the space after the number and "am" is optional. Be clear.

**Train numbers daily:** Use YouTube "IELTS listening number practice" – there are videos with 100 random numbers. Listen and write them down. Check.

## 2.6 Names: How to Spell What You Hear

In IELTS Listening Section 1, you almost always have to spell a name (a person's name, a street name, a company name).

**The audio will spell it out loud:** "My name is Brian – that's B-R-I-A-N."

**Common traps for Nigerians:**
- **B/V confusion:** "Brian" vs "Vrian" (no, 'Vrian' is not a name). Listen carefully.
- **S/C confusion:** "Sindy" vs "Cindy" – both exist. The audio will say "C for Charlie".
- **Double letters:** "Anna" (two n's), "Robert" (one 'r' at the end? No – R-o-b-e-r-t – one 'r' in the middle? Actually two? Wait: R-o-b-e-r-t – only one 'r' after 'e'? No, R-o-b-e-r-t – that's 'r' then 't'. It's fine. Just listen.)

**Tip:** When the audio spells a name, repeat it silently in your head **with the same letters**. Do not trust your assumption.

**Practice:** Listen to any English podcast. When they mention a name, pause and try to spell it. Then check online.

## 2.7 Accents: Australian, British, Canadian – What Nigerians Miss

Most Nigerians are comfortable with British and American accents. But IELTS uses Australian, New Zealand, and sometimes South African or Indian accents.

**Why this matters:**
- Australian English: "day" sounds like "die", "no" sounds like "naur".
- Canadian English: "about" sounds like "aboot" (subtle), "process" is pronounced "pro-cess" (long 'o').

**How to train for accents (free):**
- Watch **Australian news** (ABC Australia on YouTube) – 10 minutes daily.
- Watch **Canadian TV** (CBC News).
- Use **BBC World Service** (already British, but good baseline).

**Specific Nigerian tip:** Do not assume that a non-British accent is "wrong". The answer is still the same word – just said differently. Focus on the sound, not your expectation.

## 2.8 The Band 9 Listening Strategy (Step-by-Step)

Here is your exact moment-by-moment plan for each section of the Listening test.

**Before the audio starts (30 seconds):**
1. Read the title of the section (tells you the context).
2. Look at the first 5 questions. Predict answer types (write symbols: #, N, adj).
3. Underline keywords (names, dates, places – things that can't be changed).

**While listening (first play):**
- Keep your eyes on the **next question** – not the one you just answered.
- Write your answer as you hear it. Abbreviate if needed (e.g., "accomm" for accommodation) – you will copy it properly at the end.
- If you miss one answer, **move on immediately**. Do not panic. One wrong is okay (Band 9 allows 1 error). Two wrong in a row is fatal.

**After the section ends (30 seconds):**
- Go back and **check spelling**, **plurals**, and **capitalisation** (names need capital letters).
- Expand your abbreviations.

**At the very end (10 minutes transfer time):**
- You get 10 minutes after the audio finishes to transfer answers to the answer sheet.
- **Use all 10 minutes.** Do not rush.
- Check every answer for:
  - Spelling (especially common Nigerian misspellings)
  - Plurals (did the audio say 's'?)
  - Capital letters (start of sentences? Proper names?)
  - Number format (15 not 50)

## 2.9 Common Trap Questions (And How to Avoid Them)

Trap questions are designed to catch you if you stop listening too early.

**Trap 1: The correction**
- Audio: "We will meet at 3pm. Oh no, that's too early. Let's make it 4pm."
- Question: *The meeting time is ______ .*
- Answer: 4pm (not 3pm). You must hear the correction.

**Trap 2: Two possible answers, one is extra information**
- Audio: "You need a blue pen or a black pen. But actually, only blue pens are allowed."
- Question: *The required pen colour is ______ .*
- Answer: blue.

**Trap 3: The speaker changes their mind**
- Audio: "I thought the answer was chapter 5, but then I realised it's chapter 7."
- Answer: chapter 7.

**How to avoid traps:** Never write the first number or name you hear. Wait for confirmation or final decision. IELTS nearly always has a correction in Section 1 and Section 3.

## 2.11 Map/Plan/Diagram Labelling – The Nigerian Blind Spot

Many Nigerians find map and diagram labelling difficult because it requires **spatial listening** – following directions while looking at a visual.

**The format:** You will see a map (e.g., of a park, a museum, a university campus) or a diagram (e.g., a machine, a tool). There will be blank labels (e.g., "Reception ______"). You listen to a monologue or conversation and fill in the blanks.

**Why Nigerians struggle:**
- We are used to linear listening (one idea after another). Maps require you to move your eyes around the page while listening.
- Direction words like "north", "south", "opposite", "behind", "adjacent to" can be confusing.

**Key vocabulary for maps (memorise these):**

| Word/Phrase | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **north / south / east / west** | cardinal directions | "The café is north of the library." |
| **north-east, south-west, etc.** | diagonal directions | "The car park is south-west of the entrance." |
| **opposite** | directly across from | "The toilets are opposite the information desk." |
| **behind** | at the back of | "The garden is behind the main building." |
| **in front of** | directly before | "The statue is in front of the museum." |
| **adjacent to / next to** | beside | "The gift shop is adjacent to the exit." |
| **at the end of** | final point on a path | "The auditorium is at the end of the corridor." |
| **corner** | where two walls/paths meet | "The phone booth is on the left-hand corner." |
| **roundabout** | circular junction | "Take the second exit at the roundabout." |

**Step-by-step strategy for maps (Band 9 method):**

1. **Before listening:** Look at the map. Find the **starting point** (often marked with an arrow or a box saying "You are here").
2. **Identify fixed landmarks:** Circle any labelled places (e.g., "Main Entrance", "Car Park A"). These are your anchors.
3. **Predict the blanks:** For each blank, guess what kind of place it might be (e.g., "a café", "a toilet", "an exit").
4. **While listening:** Keep one finger on the starting point. Move your finger as the speaker gives directions. Do not look away from the map for more than 1 second.
5. **Listen for transition words:** "First", "next", "then", "after that", "finally" – these tell you the order of answers.

**Example from real IELTS (simplified):**

*Map: A zoo. Labels: blank 1 (next to the lion enclosure), blank 2 (opposite the café).*

Audio: *"If you look at the map, you'll see the lion enclosure in the north. Just next to it, on the eastern side, is the elephant habitat. That's blank 1. Now, if you walk south, you'll reach the café. Directly opposite the café, across the path, you'll find the souvenir shop. That's blank 2."*

Answers: 1 = elephant habitat, 2 = souvenir shop.

**Exercise:** Draw a simple map of your neighbourhood. Label 5 places. Then describe the route from your house to each place using the vocabulary above. Record yourself. Listen back.

## 2.12 Multiple Choice with 3 Options (A, B, C)

Multiple choice in IELTS Listening is harder than it looks because:
- The audio will mention **all three options** (distractors).
- You have to choose the **correct one** based on the full context.

**The Band 9 trick:** Do not listen for the correct answer first. Instead, listen for what **eliminates** the wrong answers.

**Example:**

Question: *What does the speaker say about the new library?*  
A. It has more computers than the old library.  
B. It is open on Sundays.  
C. It was built last year.

Audio: *"Our new library, which opened two years ago, has a similar number of computers as the old one. However, the opening hours have improved – it is now open every Sunday from 10am to 4pm."*

- Option A is wrong: "similar number" (not more).
- Option B is correct: "open every Sunday".
- Option C is wrong: "opened two years ago" (not last year).

**How to handle MCQ without getting lost:**

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Read all options before the audio starts. Underline key words (differences between A, B, C). |
| 2 | As you listen, put a small dot next to any option that is mentioned. |
| 3 | Listen for **rejection words** ("but", "however", "actually", "although", "rather than"). These often signal that a previous option is wrong. |
| 4 | At the end of the section, choose the option that was **confirmed** and not rejected. |

**Common trap:** The audio mentions option A first, then says "but that's not the case", then mentions option B, then says "the real reason is C". If you stop listening after A or B, you will get it wrong.

**Practice:** Use any IELTS Listening MCQ set. Do not look at the answers. Play the audio once. Circle your answers. Play again with the transcript. See where you were tricked.

## 2.13 Short Answer Questions – Word Limits

In some sections, you will see questions like:

*Write **NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER** for each answer.*

**Why Nigerians fail here:** They write three words. Or they write a whole sentence. Or they use words that are not in the audio.

**Rules for word limits:**

| Limitation | What it means | Correct answer | Wrong answer (too many words) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ONE WORD ONLY** | Exactly one word. No numbers. No hyphenated words (unless the hyphenated word is considered one word). | "accommodation" | "student accommodation" |
| **NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS** | One word or two words. Numbers count as one word (e.g., "15" is one word). | "bus stop" | "the bus stop" (three words) |
| **NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER** | You can have two words, or a number, or a number + two words. | "15 minutes" | "fifteen minutes" (fifteen is a word – use "15") |

**Critical tip:** If the answer is "15 minutes", do NOT write "fifteen minutes" unless the question allows words. "Fifteen" is a word. "15" is a number. Use the number form whenever possible to save words.

**Another tip:** Do not add articles (a/an/the) unless they are essential. If the audio says "the library", the answer is "library" (one word). Adding "the" might exceed the limit or be marked incorrect.

**Exercise (answers at end of unit):**

You hear: *"Students are required to submit their assignments by Friday at 5pm."*

Question (NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER): *Submission deadline: ______ .*

What do you write?

# UNIT 3: READING FOR BAND 9

## 3.1 The Reading Test – What Nigerians Get Wrong

The IELTS Academic Reading test has 40 questions, 3 passages, 60 minutes. Passages are long (800–1000 words each) and come from books, journals, magazines, or newspapers.

**Why Nigerians lose points (examiner reports):**

| Problem | How it hurts your score |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Time management** | Spending 30 minutes on Passage 1 (easy), then rushing Passage 3 (hard). |
| **True/False/Not Given** | The most failed question type. Nigerians often confuse "False" with "Not Given". |
| **Reading every word** | You cannot read 3000+ words in 60 minutes and answer 40 questions. You must skim and scan. |
| **Vocabulary gaps** | Academic words (e.g., "consequently", "nevertheless", "corresponding") cause confusion. |
| **Not understanding paraphrasing** | The passage says "the car is red". The question says "the vehicle is crimson". Same meaning, different words. If you don't spot paraphrasing, you miss the answer. |

**The Band 9 mindset:** Reading is not a reading test. It is a **search test**. You are looking for specific information. You do not need to understand every word.

---

## 3.2 Skimming and Scanning – The Nigerian Way

**Skimming** = reading quickly for the main idea (1–2 minutes per passage).  
**Scanning** = searching for specific words or numbers (like looking for a name in a phone book).

**How to skim a passage (Band 9 method):**
1. Read the **title** and **subheadings** (if any).
2. Read the **first sentence** of each paragraph (topic sentence).
3. Look at any **bold**, *italic*, or **capitalised** words.
4. Look at **numbers**, **dates**, or **names**.
5. Ignore examples (e.g., "for instance...") unless the question asks for them.

Do this in **60–90 seconds**. You will then know where each type of information is located.

**How to scan for answers:**
- Identify a **keyword** from the question (a name, date, or unusual word that cannot be paraphrased).
- Run your finger down the passage looking for that exact word or a synonym.
- When you find it, read the surrounding 2–3 sentences carefully.

**Example:**  
Question: *What did the 2017 study conclude about sleep?*  
Keyword: "2017". Scan for "2017" in the passage. Found in paragraph 3. Read that paragraph.

**Practice:** Take any newspaper article. Time yourself 60 seconds to skim. Then close the article and write down three things you remember. Do this daily for one week.

---

## 3.3 True / False / Not Given – The Decoder

This question type causes the most frustration. Let us break it down with Nigerian-friendly rules.

**The three options mean:**

| Option | Meaning | In simple terms |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **TRUE** | The passage says exactly what the question says (using synonyms). | The passage agrees with the question. |
| **FALSE** | The passage says the **opposite** of the question. | The passage contradicts the question. |
| **NOT GIVEN** | The passage does not mention this idea at all. | You cannot find any evidence either way. |

**The biggest mistake:** Choosing "False" when the passage simply does not mention the idea. That is "Not Given".

**Example passage:**

*"The conference was held in Lagos. Only three speakers attended. The main topic was climate change."*

| Question | Answer | Why? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| The conference took place in Lagos. | TRUE | Passage says exactly that. |
| The conference was held in Abuja. | FALSE | Passage says Lagos, which is the opposite of Abuja. |
| The conference had four speakers. | FALSE | Passage says three speakers. Four is opposite. |
| The conference was free to attend. | NOT GIVEN | Passage does not mention cost. |
| Climate change was discussed. | TRUE | Passage says "main topic was climate change". |

**Nigerian tip:** If you cannot find the information after 30 seconds of scanning, it is probably NOT GIVEN. Do not waste time searching endlessly.

**Quick exercise (answers at end of unit):**

Passage: *"Okonkwo was a famous writer from Nigeria. He published his first book in 1958. Many critics consider him the father of African literature."*

Questions:
1. Okonkwo was born in 1958. (T/F/NG)
2. Okonkwo wrote more than ten books. (T/F/NG)
3. Critics admire Okonkwo's work. (T/F/NG)

---

## 3.4 Matching Headings to Paragraphs

You are given a list of headings (e.g., "The causes of pollution", "Historical background", "Future solutions"). You must match each heading to the correct paragraph.

**Strategy that works:**
1. Read the headings first. Underline key words.
2. Skim the first paragraph. Ask yourself: *What is the main idea?* Not the details.
3. Look at the headings again. Which one best summarises this paragraph?
4. Eliminate used headings as you go. The same heading cannot be used twice (unless the instructions say otherwise – usually they don't).

**Trap:** Do not match a heading just because it contains a word that appears in the paragraph. That is often a distractor. You need the **main idea** of the whole paragraph, not a single sentence.

**Example:**

Paragraph: *"The Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the late 18th century. Factories replaced manual labour. Coal became the primary energy source. Railways expanded rapidly. These changes transformed society and the economy."*

Possible headings:
A. The role of coal in modern industry
B. Social and economic transformation in 18th-century Britain
C. How factories replaced manual labour

Which one is correct? **B** – because it captures the entire paragraph (society AND economy, 18th century, transformation). A is too narrow (only coal). C is too narrow (only factories).

---

## 3.5 Sentence Completion (No More Than Two/Three Words)

Similar to Listening, you will fill in blanks using words from the passage. Word limits apply.

**The Band 9 trick:** Find the **exact words** in the passage. Do not paraphrase. If the passage says "photosynthesis is a process", and the question says "Photosynthesis is a ______", the answer is "process" (one word), not "chemical process" unless "chemical" is also in the passage in that exact phrase.

**How to locate answers:**
1. Read the sentence with the blank. Identify keywords (nouns, verbs, numbers).
2. Scan the passage for those keywords or synonyms.
3. Find the sentence in the passage that contains similar meaning.
4. Copy the missing words **exactly** as they appear (same spelling, same word form).

**Example:**

Passage: *"The fastest land animal is the cheetah, which can reach speeds of up to 120 kilometres per hour."*

Question: *The cheetah can run at a maximum speed of ______ .* (NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER)

Answer: "120 kilometres" (or "120 km" – but the passage says "120 kilometres per hour" – you cannot write "per hour" because that would be three words? Actually "120 kilometres" is two words. "120 kilometres per hour" is four words – too many. So the correct answer is "120 kilometres". The question asks for "maximum speed" – the unit "kilometres" is sufficient. However, the safest is "120 km/h" – but the passage does not have "/h". Better to write "120 kilometres".)

**Official answer key would accept:** "120 kilometres" or "120 km" (if km is considered a number+abbreviation). To be safe, use the exact words from the passage within the word limit.

## 3.6 Time Management – The 20-20-20 Rule

Most Nigerians run out of time on Passage 3. Here is the exact time split for Band 9:

| Passage | Difficulty | Time allocated | Target correct |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Passage 1 | Easiest | 15 minutes | 13/13 or 12/13 |
| Passage 2 | Medium | 20 minutes | 12/13 or 11/13 |
| Passage 3 | Hardest | 25 minutes | 12/14 or 11/14 |

**Total:** 60 minutes. Aim for 35–38 correct.

**What to do if you are stuck on a question:**
- Spend maximum 1 minute on any question.
- If you cannot find the answer, guess and move on.
- Put a small mark next to the question (e.g., "?") so you can return if there is time at the end.

**Do not:** Leave any answer blank. There is no penalty for guessing. Always fill in all 40 boxes.

## 3.7 Summary Completion (Flowcharts, Tables, Notes)

In this question type, you are given a summary of part of the passage with blanks. You must fill each blank with words taken directly from the passage.

**Two versions:**
- **With a word bank** (choose from a list) – easier.
- **Without a word bank** (you find the words yourself) – harder.

**Band 9 strategy (without word bank):**
1. Read the summary first. Do not read the passage yet.
2. Identify the **topic** of the summary (e.g., "the process of photosynthesis").
3. Predict the **type of word** needed for each blank (noun, verb, adjective, number).
4. Scan the passage for the section that matches the summary's topic.
5. Find the exact words that fit grammatically and logically.

**Example:**

Passage excerpt: *"To apply for a passport, a Nigerian citizen must first complete Form A online. Next, they must schedule an appointment at a passport office. After attending the appointment, the applicant will receive their passport within two to three weeks."*

Summary: *The passport application process begins with completing ______ online. Then, an ______ must be booked. Finally, the passport is issued after ______ weeks.*

Answers: "Form A" (noun), "appointment" (noun), "two to three" (number phrase).

**Flowcharts:** These show a sequence (step 1 → step 2 → step 3). Follow the order in the passage. The answers will appear in the same order as the flowchart.

**Tables:** These have rows and columns. Look for headings (e.g., "Time", "Event", "Location"). Scan the passage for information that matches the row/column categories.

**Common Nigerian error:** Writing an answer that is grammatically incorrect in the blank. For example, if the summary says "the ______ of the study", the blank must be a noun (e.g., "results"), not a verb ("concluded"). Always check grammar.

---

## 3.8 Multiple Choice (Reading Version)

Reading multiple choice is different from Listening multiple choice. In Reading, you have more time to check back with the passage.

**The four common traps:**

| Trap | Example | How to avoid |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Opposite meaning** | Passage says "increased", option says "decreased" | Check for negation words (not, never, opposite) |
| **Partially true** | Option has 3 correct words and 1 wrong word | Read every word of the option. All must be true. |
| **Not mentioned** | Option introduces a new idea not in passage | If not in passage, it's wrong. Eliminate. |
| **Too specific** | Passage gives a general statement, option gives a narrow exception | The correct answer will match the passage's scope. |

**Step-by-step method:**
1. Read the question and all options.
2. Underline keywords in each option.
3. Scan the passage for the relevant section.
4. Eliminate options that are clearly false or not mentioned.
5. Between the last two options, find the sentence in the passage that directly supports one of them.
6. Choose that one.

**Example:**

Passage: *"While many researchers believe that climate change is primarily caused by human activity, a minority argue that natural cycles play a larger role. However, the overwhelming consensus supports the human-caused theory."*

Question: *What does the passage say about climate change?*
A. All researchers agree it is caused by natural cycles.
B. Most researchers attribute it to human activity.
C. Natural cycles are the main cause according to the majority.
D. There is no consensus among researchers.

Analysis:
- A is false: "all" is wrong (passage says minority disagree).
- B is true: "overwhelming consensus supports human-caused".
- C is false: that's the minority view.
- D is false: there is a consensus (overwhelming).

Answer: **B**

---

## 3.9 Locating Information (Which Paragraph Contains...)

This question gives you a piece of information (e.g., "a reason for the decline in bee populations") and asks you to find which paragraph (A, B, C, D...) contains that information.

**Critical difference:** You are not matching headings (main idea). You are matching **specific details**. A paragraph may have many details.

**Band 9 strategy:**
1. Read all the information statements (there are usually 5–7).
2. Underline the **key noun phrase** in each statement.
3. Start with the easiest statement (one that has a number, date, name, or unusual word).
4. Scan each paragraph quickly for that keyword or a clear synonym.
5. When you find it, write the paragraph letter.
6. Eliminate that paragraph from further searching (each paragraph may be used once, more than once, or not at all – read instructions carefully).

**Trap:** Some paragraphs will contain similar but not identical information. For example, the statement says "the benefits of exercise" but the paragraph only mentions "physical activity" without saying "benefits". That is not a match. Be precise.

**Example:**

Paragraph A: *"Regular walking can lower blood pressure and reduce stress."*
Paragraph B: *"Many people do not exercise because they lack time."*
Paragraph C: *"Gyms have become more affordable in recent years."*

Information statements:
1. A barrier to physical activity. → **B** (lack of time)
2. Positive effects of walking. → **A** (lower blood pressure, reduce stress)
3. Cost changes in fitness centres. → **C** (gyms more affordable)

## 3.10 Dealing with Difficult Vocabulary (Inferring Meaning)

You will encounter unknown words in the Reading passages. At Band 9, you must be able to guess meaning from context without a dictionary.

**Five clues to find meaning:**

| Clue | What to look for | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | The passage defines the word directly. | "Photosynthesis, the process by which plants make food, occurs in leaves." |
| **Synonym** | A similar word appears nearby. | "The weather was inclement – cold, wet, and windy." |
| **Antonym** | An opposite word gives contrast. | "Unlike his loquacious brother, he was silent." (loquacious = talkative) |
| **Example** | Examples illustrate the word. | "Nocturnal animals, such as bats and owls, are active at night." (nocturnal = night-active) |
| **Word parts** | Prefixes/suffixes give hints. | "Substandard" – sub (below) + standard → below standard. |

**Nigerian tip:** Do not panic when you see a difficult word. Very often, the question does not require you to understand that exact word. It is there to distract you. Focus on the words you do know.

**Exercise:** Guess the meaning of the underlined word from context:

*"The ancient manuscript was **illegible**; centuries of water damage had made the ink impossible to read."*

Meaning: ______________ (Answer: unreadable)

## 3.11 5 Full Reading Passages with Answers and Explanations

(The full textbook would contain 5 complete Reading passages. Due to space, I am providing **one full passage** as a model. You can find 4 more in the Cambridge IELTS books or online.)

**Passage: The History of Chocolate (approximately 700 words)**

Chocolate originates from the cacao tree, which is native to the Amazon basin. The Olmec civilization of ancient Mexico was the first to turn cacao into a drink, around 1500 BCE. They fermented, roasted, and ground the beans into a paste, then mixed it with water and spices.

The Mayans and Aztecs later adopted cacao. For the Aztecs, cacao beans were so valuable that they were used as currency. The Aztec emperor Montezuma II was said to drink 50 cups of cacao per day from a golden goblet.

When Spanish conquistadors arrived in the Americas in the 16th century, they initially found the cacao drink bitter and unpleasant. However, after adding sugar and honey, it became popular in Spain. For nearly a century, Spain kept cacao a secret from the rest of Europe.

By the 17th century, chocolate houses had opened in London, Paris, and Amsterdam. These establishments were similar to coffee houses and became centres of social and political discussion. Chocolate was still consumed as a drink; solid chocolate for eating was not invented until 1847 by the British company J.S. Fry & Sons.

Today, the global chocolate industry is worth over $100 billion annually. However, concerns about child labour and deforestation in West African cacao farms have led to calls for ethical certification.

**Questions (True/False/Not Given):**

1. The Olmec civilization was the first to use cacao for drinking. (T/F/NG)
2. Montezuma II drank cacao from a wooden cup. (T/F/NG)
3. Spanish conquistadors liked cacao immediately. (T/F/NG)
4. Chocolate houses in London were used for political discussions. (T/F/NG)
5. Solid chocolate was invented in 1847 by a French company. (T/F/NG)
6. West Africa produces most of the world's cacao. (T/F/NG)

**Answers and explanations:**

1. **TRUE** – Passage says "The Olmec civilization...was the first to turn cacao into a drink."
2. **FALSE** – Passage says "from a golden goblet" (not wooden).
3. **FALSE** – Passage says "initially found the cacao drink bitter and unpleasant."
4. **TRUE** – Passage says "became centres of social and political discussion."
5. **FALSE** – Passage says "by the British company J.S. Fry & Sons" (not French).
6. **NOT GIVEN** – Passage mentions West African cacao farms but does not say that West Africa produces *most* of the world's cacao.

# UNIT 4: WRITING TASK 1 – ACADEMIC

## 4.1 What Task 1 Is (And Is Not)

In Academic IELTS Writing Task 1, you are given a visual (graph, chart, table, diagram, map, or process) and you must write **at least 150 words** in about **20 minutes**.

**What Task 1 is:**
- A factual report describing the main features of the visual.
- Objective (no opinions, no conclusions, no recommendations).
- Organized (clear paragraphs: introduction, overview, details).

**What Task 1 is NOT:**
- An essay (do not write "I think", "in my opinion", "this shows that").
- A story (do not describe every single data point).
- A place to use flowery language (be clear and precise).

**Scoring:** Task 1 is worth 1/3 of your Writing band. Task 2 is worth 2/3. So do not spend more than 20 minutes on Task 1.

**The biggest Nigerian mistake:** Spending 30–35 minutes on Task 1 because you want to describe everything perfectly. Then you have only 25 minutes for Task 2 and you panic. **Do not do this.** Finish Task 1 in 20 minutes maximum.

---

## 4.2 The 4-Paragraph Structure That Works Every Time

Memorise this structure. Use it for every Task 1 type (except maps/processes – small variation).

| Paragraph | Content | Approx. number of sentences |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Paragraph 1: Introduction** | Paraphrase the question. Say what the visual shows (what, where, when). | 1–2 sentences |
| **Paragraph 2: Overview** | The most important paragraph. Describe 2–3 big-picture trends or key features. Do not give numbers here. | 2–3 sentences |
| **Paragraph 3: Details (first group)** | Specific data, comparisons, or changes. Support the overview. | 3–5 sentences |
| **Paragraph 4: Details (second group)** | More specific data. Usually contrasts with or adds to paragraph 3. | 2–4 sentences |

**No conclusion paragraph.** Task 1 does not need a conclusion. The overview acts as the summary.

**Example using a simple line graph (population growth of two cities):**

*Question: The graph shows the population of Lagos and Abuja between 2000 and 2020.*

**Introduction (paraphrase):**  
*The line graph compares the population changes in two Nigerian cities, Lagos and Abuja, over a 20-year period from 2000 to 2020.*

**Overview (key trends – no numbers):**  
*Overall, both cities experienced significant population growth. However, Lagos consistently had a much larger population than Abuja throughout the period, while Abuja saw a more rapid rate of increase.*

**Details (group 1 – Lagos):**  
*In 2000, Lagos had approximately 10 million residents. This figure rose steadily over the next two decades, reaching about 15 million in 2010 and then climbing sharply to nearly 25 million by 2020.*

**Details (group 2 – Abuja):**  
*By contrast, Abuja's population was only around 1 million in 2000. Nevertheless, it showed a dramatic rise, tripling to 3 million by 2010 and then doubling again to 6 million in 2020. Although Abuja's growth rate was faster, its total population remained far smaller than Lagos's.*

That is a Band 9 response. Notice: no opinion, no "I think", just facts.

---

## 4.3 Paraphrasing for the Introduction

You must **paraphrase** the question. Do not copy it word for word.

**How to paraphrase (5 techniques):**

| Technique | Example (original) | Paraphrased |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Change synonyms** | "The graph shows" | "The line graph illustrates" |
| **Change word order** | "Population of Lagos and Abuja" | "Lagos and Abuja's population" |
| **Change grammar (active/passive)** | "The chart compares..." | "A comparison is made by the chart..." (but this can sound awkward – use sparingly) |
| **Change word form** | "Between 2000 and 2020" | "From 2000 to 2020" or "Over a 20-year period" |
| **Add context** | (no original) | "Over a 20-year period from 2000 to 2020" |

**Common paraphrasing mistakes by Nigerians:**
- Changing the meaning: "The graph shows" → "The graph indicates" is fine. "The graph guesses" is wrong.
- Using incorrect synonyms: "City" → "urban centre" is okay. "City" → "municipality" is too formal and often wrong.
- Copying more than 3 words in a row from the question. That is plagiarism in IELTS. Always change at least the verb and the word order.

**Exercise:** Paraphrase this question: *"The table below gives information about the average monthly rainfall in three Nigerian cities: Lagos, Kano, and Port Harcourt."*

Write your paraphrase in your notebook. Then compare with a sample: *"The provided table presents data on the mean monthly precipitation recorded in Lagos, Kano, and Port Harcourt, three cities in Nigeria."*

---

## 4.4 Writing the Overview (The Most Important Paragraph)

Examiners say: *"If you do not write an overview, your Task Response score cannot go above 5.0."*

The overview is **one paragraph** (2–3 sentences) that identifies the **most significant trends or features** without giving any numbers.

**What counts as a "significant feature"?**
- Highest / lowest values
- Biggest increase / decrease
- Similarities or contrasts
- Changes over time (increase, decrease, fluctuate, remain stable)
- Rankings (A is larger than B, C is the smallest)

**Overview sentence starters (use these naturally):**
- *Overall, it is clear that...*
- *In general, there was a significant...*
- *Looking at the overall picture,...*
- *It is evident that...*

**Example for a bar chart showing internet usage by age group:**

Bad overview (gives numbers): *Overall, people aged 18-25 used the internet for 8 hours per day, while those over 60 used it for only 1 hour.*

Good overview (no numbers): *Overall, younger age groups spent considerably more time online than older groups. Internet usage declined steadily with increasing age.*

**Practice:** Look at any chart. Cover the numbers. Write two sentences describing only the patterns. Then uncover the numbers. Did your overview match reality? If yes, you are ready.

## 4.5 Describing Data: Vocabulary for Trends and Comparisons

You need precise vocabulary to describe changes (line graphs) and comparisons (bar charts, pie charts, tables).

**Vocabulary for changes over time (line graphs):**

| Direction | Degree | Verbs | Nouns (used with "there was a...") |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Up** | Sharp | soared, surged, shot up, rocketed | a sharp rise, a dramatic increase, a surge |
| **Up** | Moderate | rose, increased, grew, climbed | a rise, an increase, growth, an upward trend |
| **Up** | Slight | edged up, rose slightly | a slight increase, a modest rise |
| **Down** | Sharp | plummeted, plunged, collapsed, fell sharply | a sharp fall, a dramatic decline, a plunge |
| **Down** | Moderate | decreased, declined, dropped, fell | a decrease, a decline, a drop, a downward trend |
| **Down** | Slight | dipped, fell slightly | a slight dip, a minor decline |
| **Stable** | No change | remained stable, stayed constant, plateaued | stability, a plateau |
| **Fluctuating** | Up and down | fluctuated, varied | fluctuations, variations |

**Vocabulary for comparisons (bar charts, pie charts, tables):**

| Comparison | Example phrase |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Higher/lower** | *X was significantly higher than Y.* |
| **Similar** | *X and Y were roughly similar.* |
| **The highest** | *X accounted for the largest proportion.* |
| **The lowest** | *Y represented the smallest share.* |
| **Ranking** | *X was the most common, followed by Y and then Z.* |
| **Difference** | *There was a considerable gap between X and Y.* |
| **Proportion** | *X made up three-quarters of the total.* |

**Nigerian tip:** Do not use the same verb more than once in the same paragraph. If you write "increased" in sentence 1, use "rose" or "grew" in sentence 2. Variety shows vocabulary range.

**Exercise:** Write two sentences describing the following data (make up numbers if needed):  
- A line graph showing petrol prices in Nigeria from 2010 to 2020.  
- A bar chart comparing the number of universities in each Nigerian state.

Write your sentences, then check: Did you use a trend verb? Did you avoid numbers in the overview? Did you vary your vocabulary?

## 4.6 Describing Percentages and Fractions (For Pie Charts & Tables)

Pie charts and tables often use percentages. You need to describe them accurately without repeating "percent" ten times.

**Ways to express percentages:**

| Percentage | Alternative phrase |
| :--- | :--- |
| 100% | the entire / all / the whole |
| 75–80% | the vast majority / three-quarters / a large proportion |
| 60–70% | the majority / more than half / a significant proportion |
| 50% | half / exactly half / one in two |
| 40–49% | a large minority / just under half / nearly half |
| 25–30% | a quarter / a minority / approximately one-third (for 33%) |
| 10–20% | a small minority / one in ten / a fraction |
| 5% or less | a tiny fraction / a negligible proportion / very few |

**Example pie chart description (without repeating "%"):**

*"The pie chart shows that **the vast majority** of Nigerian internet users (78%) access the web via smartphones. **A small minority** (12%) use laptops, while tablets and desktop computers account for **only a tiny fraction** (5% and 5% respectively)."*

**Common Nigerian error:** Writing "78 percent" in full. Use the symbol "%" – it saves time and is perfectly acceptable. Do not write "78 %" with a space; write "78%" without a space.

**For fractions:** Use words for common fractions (one-third, two-thirds, three-quarters) and numbers for less common ones (7/20 is fine as "7 out of 20" or "35%").

**Exercise:** Rewrite these without using the word "percent":
1. 45% of respondents preferred coffee.
2. 3% of the budget was spent on advertising.
3. 67% of students passed the exam.

(Answers: 1. Just under half of respondents preferred coffee. 2. A negligible proportion of the budget was spent on advertising. 3. Approximately two-thirds of students passed the exam.)

## 4.7 Line Graphs (Step-by-Step with Model Answer)

Line graphs show changes over time. The x-axis is usually time (years, months, hours). The y-axis is a quantity (number, percentage, value).

**Step-by-step method for Band 9:**

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Look at the title and axes. Identify: what is being measured? Over what period? What units? |
| 2 | Identify the overall trend for each line (up, down, fluctuating, stable). |
| 3 | Write your **introduction** (paraphrase). |
| 4 | Write your **overview**: the most obvious patterns (e.g., "all lines increased", "one line rose sharply while the other fell"). |
| 5 | Write **details paragraphs**: group lines that move together. Describe starting points, ending points, peaks, troughs, and any significant changes (steep slopes, plateaus). |
| 6 | Use a range of trend vocabulary (soared, plummeted, fluctuated, etc.). |

**Model Answer (Line Graph):**

**Question:** *The line graph shows the number of international tourists visiting three Nigerian cities (Lagos, Abuja, Calabar) between 2010 and 2020.*

**Introduction (paraphrased):**  
*The line graph illustrates changes in the volume of foreign tourists to Lagos, Abuja, and Calabar over an 11-year period from 2010 to 2020.*

**Overview:**  
*Overall, all three cities saw an increase in tourist numbers over the decade. Lagos consistently attracted the highest number of visitors, while Calabar experienced the most dramatic growth.*

**Details (Lagos and Abuja):**  
*In 2010, Lagos received approximately 500,000 tourists, far exceeding Abuja's 200,000. Both cities showed a steady upward trend until 2015, when Lagos reached 700,000 and Abuja 350,000. From 2015 to 2020, Lagos's growth accelerated sharply, hitting 1.2 million by the end of the period. Abuja also continued to rise but more modestly, finishing at 450,000.*

**Details (Calabar):**  
*Calabar started at just 50,000 tourists in 2010, the lowest of the three. However, it then experienced a remarkable surge, overtaking Abuja in 2018 and reaching 600,000 by 2020. Although Calabar's final figure was still half that of Lagos, its percentage increase was the largest.*

**Word count:** 168 (perfect)

## 4.8 Bar Charts (Step-by-Step with Model Answer)

Bar charts compare categories (e.g., different countries, age groups, products). Bars can be vertical or horizontal. There may be one bar per category or multiple bars grouped.

**Step-by-step method:**

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Identify the categories and the measured variable. |
| 2 | Look for the highest, lowest, and any surprising similarities or differences. |
| 3 | Write introduction (paraphrase). |
| 4 | Write overview: the main comparisons (e.g., "X was by far the largest", "Y and Z were roughly similar"). |
| 5 | Write details: group similar bars together. Use comparison language (higher than, lower than, three times as many, etc.). |

**Model Answer (Bar Chart – grouping categories by similarity):**

**Question:** *The bar chart compares the average weekly hours spent on leisure activities by age group in Nigeria.*

**Introduction:**  
*The bar chart provides information on how many hours per week different age groups in Nigeria spend on various leisure activities.*

**Overview:**  
*Overall, younger Nigerians spent considerably more time on leisure than older groups. Watching television was the most popular activity across all ages, while reading was the least common.*

**Details (younger vs older):**  
*Nigerians aged 18–30 spent the most time on leisure, averaging 35 hours per week. This figure dropped steadily with age, falling to 20 hours for the 46–60 group and just 12 hours for those over 60. Social media usage showed a similar pattern: 10 hours for the youngest group, compared to only 1 hour for the oldest.*

**Details (activity preferences):**  
*Television viewing was consistently high, ranging from 15 hours (youngest) to 8 hours (oldest). Sports participation peaked in the 31–45 age group at 5 hours per week, while reading never exceeded 2 hours for any age group, with the elderly reading the least.*

## 4.9 Pie Charts (Step-by-Step with Model Answer)

Pie charts show proportions of a whole. The entire pie equals 100%. You may have one pie chart or multiple pie charts for comparison (e.g., 2000 vs 2020).

**Step-by-step method:**

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Identify what the whole represents (e.g., "total energy consumption"). |
| 2 | Identify the largest segment, the smallest, and any segments that are roughly equal. |
| 3 | Write introduction (paraphrase). |
| 4 | Write overview: mention the dominant segment(s) and any notable contrasts. |
| 5 | Write details: compare segments using fractions or percentages. For two pie charts, highlight changes over time. |

**Model Answer (Two pie charts comparing years):**

**Question:** *The pie charts show the percentage of Nigerian households with access to different energy sources in 2000 and 2020.*

**Introduction:**  
*The two pie charts compare the energy sources used by Nigerian households in 2000 and 2020.*

**Overview:**  
*Overall, the reliance on traditional biomass (firewood) decreased significantly, while electricity and solar power became much more common. Electricity emerged as the dominant source by 2020.*

**Details (2000):**  
*In 2000, firewood accounted for the vast majority (70%) of household energy. Kerosene was the second most used source at 15%, followed by electricity at only 10%. Solar and other sources made up the remaining 5%.*

**Details (2020 and changes):**  
*By 2020, the pattern had shifted dramatically. Electricity rose to 45%, becoming the largest segment. Firewood fell sharply to 25%, while solar power increased tenfold to 15%. Kerosene also declined to 10%. The proportion from other sources remained stable at 5%.*

## 4.10 Tables (Step-by-Step with Model Answer)

Tables present data in rows and columns. They can be overwhelming because of the amount of numbers. The key is to **select** – do not report every number.

**Step-by-step method:**

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Read the title and column/row headings. |
| 2 | Look for the highest and lowest values in each row/column. |
| 3 | Look for patterns (e.g., "as X increases, Y decreases"). |
| 4 | Write introduction (paraphrase). |
| 5 | Write overview: the most striking patterns across the table. |
| 6 | Write details: report the most important numbers, grouping similar data. Use approximate language where appropriate ("approximately", "roughly", "just over"). |

**Model Answer (Table):**

**Question:** *The table shows the literacy rates (percentage of adults who can read and write) in six Nigerian states by gender in 2020.*

| State | Male literacy (%) | Female literacy (%) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Lagos | 96 | 92 |
| Rivers | 89 | 81 |
| Kano | 72 | 58 |
| Kaduna | 68 | 52 |
| Enugu | 87 | 79 |
| Borno | 55 | 42 |

**Introduction:**  
*The table presents data on adult literacy rates for six Nigerian states in 2020, broken down by gender.*

**Overview:**  
*Overall, literacy rates varied significantly by state, with southern states showing much higher rates than northern states. Males consistently had higher literacy than females in every state, with the widest gap in the north.*

**Details (highest and lowest):**  
*Lagos recorded the highest literacy for both genders (96% for males, 92% for females). By contrast, Borno had the lowest figures, with only 55% of males and 42% of females literate – a gap of 13 percentage points. Kano and Kaduna also showed large gender disparities of 14 and 16 points respectively.*

**Details (southern states):**  
*In the southern states, literacy rates were generally above 80% for both genders. Rivers and Enugu had male literacy of 89% and 87% respectively, with corresponding female rates around 80%, resulting in narrower gender gaps (8–9 percentage points).*

## 4.11 Maps and Processes (Different Structure)

**Maps:** Usually show changes to a location over time (e.g., a town in 1990 and now). You describe what has been added, removed, or modified.

**Structure for maps:**
- Introduction (paraphrase)
- Overview (big changes: e.g., "the town expanded significantly", "the industrial area was replaced by housing")
- Details (first map / earlier period)
- Details (second map / later period, noting changes)

**Vocabulary for maps:** replaced, demolished, constructed, extended, converted into, relocated, added, removed.

**Model Answer (Maps – very condensed):**

*The maps illustrate the development of a Nigerian coastal village between 1980 and 2020. Overall, the village underwent substantial modernisation, with new infrastructure replacing natural features. In 1980, the village consisted of a few fishing huts along a sandy beach and a single dirt road. By 2020, the dirt road had been paved, a pier had been constructed, and the beach was largely replaced by a hotel complex.*

**Processes (diagrams):** Show how something works or is made (e.g., the water cycle, cement production). Use the passive voice and sequencing words.

**Structure for processes:**
- Introduction (paraphrase)
- Overview (number of stages, starting point, ending point)
- Details (describe each stage in order, using sequencing language: first, then, next, after that, finally)

**Vocabulary for processes:** is extracted, is heated, is mixed, is transported, undergoes, enters, exits, flows, turns into, becomes.

**Model Answer (Process – condensed):**

*The diagram illustrates the process of generating electricity from wind power. Overall, there are five main stages, beginning with wind turning the turbine blades and ending with electricity entering the national grid. First, wind causes the rotor blades to spin. This rotational energy is transferred to a gearbox, which increases the rotational speed. Next, the high-speed shaft drives a generator, where mechanical energy is converted into electrical energy. Finally, the electricity flows through a transformer to increase voltage before being transmitted via power lines.*

# UNIT 5: WRITING TASK 2 – THE NIGERIAN CHALLENGE

## 5.1 What Task 2 Is (And Why Nigerians Struggle Most Here)

Writing Task 2 is an **essay** of at least 250 words in 40 minutes. You are given a prompt (a question or statement) and must respond with a clear position, supported by reasons and examples.

**Why Nigerians struggle most with Task 2:**

| Reason | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **WAEC habits** | WAEC rewards long, flowery, narrative essays. IELTS rewards clear, structured, argumentative essays. |
| **Task response failure** | The most common reason Nigerians get Writing 5.5–6.5: they do not answer the exact question. |
| **Article & preposition errors** | These errors are more frequent in longer writing (essays). Each error drops your grammar score. |
| **Overcomplicated vocabulary** | Nigerian candidates often use "big words" incorrectly, sounding unnatural and sometimes wrong. |
| **Memorised phrases** | Examiners can spot memorised introductions ("This is a highly controversial topic...") and penalise them. |

**The Band 9 mindset for Task 2:** Clarity over complexity. A simple, error-free, well-organised essay that directly answers the question will score 8.0–9.0. A fancy, error-filled essay will score 6.0.

---

## 5.2 The 5 Essay Types (Memorise These)

Every IELTS Task 2 prompt falls into one of five types. You must recognise the type immediately and use the correct structure.

| Essay Type | Question keywords | What you must do |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Opinion (Agree/Disagree)** | *Do you agree or disagree? To what extent do you agree?* | State your opinion clearly. Support it with reasons. Address the opposite view. |
| **2. Discussion (Discuss both views)** | *Discuss both views and give your opinion.* | Present View A, then View B. Then give your own opinion (can be in conclusion). |
| **3. Problem / Solution** | *What are the causes? What solutions can you suggest?* | Explain causes (problems). Propose solutions. Explain how they work. |
| **4. Advantage / Disadvantage** | *Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?* | List advantages and disadvantages. Then state which is stronger. |
| **5. Double Question** | *Why is this happening? Is this a positive or negative development?* | Answer each question in a separate paragraph. |

**The biggest Nigerian mistake:** Treating every prompt as an "agree/disagree" essay. For example, a prompt that asks for "causes and solutions" is **not** an opinion essay. Do not write "I agree that..." for a problem/solution prompt. You will lose Task Response points.

**Self-test:** Identify the essay type for these prompts:

1. *"Some people believe that university education should be free for all. Others think students should pay. Discuss both views and give your opinion."* (Type: Discussion)
2. *"Many Nigerian graduates are unemployed. What are the reasons for this, and what solutions can you suggest?"* (Type: Problem/Solution)
3. *"Social media has made people less connected in real life. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"* (Type: Opinion)
4. *"Some countries have banned smoking in public places. Do the advantages of this outweigh the disadvantages?"* (Type: Advantage/Disadvantage)
5. *"More and more people are moving from rural areas to cities. Why is this happening? Is it a positive or negative trend?"* (Type: Double Question)

---

## 5.3 The 4-Paragraph Structure That Works for Almost Every Essay Type

For most essay types (Opinion, Discussion, Problem/Solution, Advantage/Disadvantage), use this structure:

| Paragraph | Content | Approx. sentences |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Paragraph 1: Introduction** | Hook (optional – paraphrase the topic), then clearly state your position or outline the essay. | 2–3 |
| **Paragraph 2: Main body (first point)** | Topic sentence, explanation, example, link back to question. | 4–5 |
| **Paragraph 3: Main body (second point)** | Topic sentence, explanation, example, link. (For discussion essays, this paragraph presents the second view.) | 4–5 |
| **Paragraph 4: Conclusion** | Summarise your main points, restate your position (using different words). No new ideas. | 2–3 |

**For Problem/Solution essays,** use two body paragraphs: one for causes/problems, one for solutions.

**For Discussion essays,** use one body paragraph for View A, one for View B, then your opinion can be in the conclusion or a separate sentence in the second body paragraph.

**For Double Question essays,** answer each question in a separate body paragraph. No need for a "position" unless the second question asks for it.

---

## 5.4 Writing the Introduction (Band 9 Method)

The introduction has two jobs:
1. Paraphrase the topic (show you understand it).
2. State your position or outline (tell the examiner what you will do).

**Bad introduction (Nigerian common – memorised and vague):**

*"In today's modern society, there is a heated debate about whether university education should be free. Some people think one way, while others think another. This essay will discuss both sides before giving my opinion."*

**Why this is bad:** It says nothing specific. "Some people think one way" – what way? The examiner already knows the topic. You are wasting words.

**Band 9 introduction for the same prompt (free university education):**

*"The question of whether tertiary education should be provided at no cost has become increasingly relevant in Nigeria, where rising tuition fees exclude many qualified students. While I acknowledge the arguments of those who oppose free university, I largely agree that making higher education free would benefit society as a whole."*

**Analysis:**
- Paraphrased the topic (university education → tertiary education; free → no cost; added Nigerian context – acceptable).
- Clear position: "I largely agree".
- Mentioned the opposing view ("while I acknowledge those who oppose").

**Formula for Band 9 introductions:**

| Sentence | Function | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Sentence 1 | Paraphrase the topic (can add slight context) | *"The issue of [topic] has attracted considerable attention, particularly in Nigeria where [relevant context]."* |
| Sentence 2 | State your position or outline | *"While there are valid arguments on both sides, I believe that..."* OR *"This essay will first examine the causes of this problem before proposing practical solutions."* |

**Do not write:** "This essay will discuss..." without telling the examiner *what* it will discuss. Be specific.

---

## 5.5 Writing Body Paragraphs – The PEEL Method

Every body paragraph should follow **PEEL**:

| Letter | Meaning | What to write |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **P** | Point | A clear topic sentence stating the main idea of the paragraph. |
| **E** | Explanation | Explain what you mean. Why is this point true? (2–3 sentences) |
| **E** | Example | Give a specific example (real or hypothetical, but realistic). Use "For example", "For instance". |
| **L** | Link | Link back to the question or to the next paragraph. |

**Example body paragraph using PEEL (topic: free university education):**

**P (Point):** *Free university education would promote greater social equality.*  
**E (Explanation):** *Currently, many talented Nigerian students from low-income families cannot afford tuition fees, which perpetuates cycles of poverty. When education is free, access is based on ability rather than financial background.*  
**E (Example):** *For example, countries like Germany and Norway have abolished tuition fees and now have some of the highest social mobility rates in the world. Students from all socioeconomic backgrounds can attend university without incurring debt.*  
**L (Link):** *Therefore, removing financial barriers not only benefits individuals but also strengthens the economy by developing a more skilled workforce.*

**Note:** The example does not have to be about Nigeria. International examples are fine. But if you can give a Nigerian example, even better.

**Nigerian tip:** Do not use "for example" for every example. Vary with "for instance", "such as", "a case in point is".

## 5.6 The "Flowery English" Trap – Why Simpler Is Better (With Before/After Examples)

This is the single most important lesson in this unit for Nigerian candidates.

**The myth:** Using big, rare, academic words makes you sound intelligent and scores higher.

**The truth:** IELTS examiners are trained to reward **precision** and **naturalness**, not complexity. When you use a fancy word incorrectly, you lose points. When you use a simple word correctly, you gain points.

**Examples of Nigerian "flowery English" vs Band 9 simple English:**

| Nigerian common (Band 6) | Band 9 simple version |
| :--- | :--- |
| *It is indubitably evident that the phenomenon of unemployment is a pernicious and deleterious predicament.* | *Unemployment is a serious problem that harms both individuals and the economy.* |
| *The government should endeavour to ameliorate the living conditions of the citizenry.* | *The government should work to improve living conditions for its citizens.* |
| *This essay will delineate the multifarious causes of this issue.* | *This essay will examine several causes of this problem.* |
| *Notwithstanding the aforementioned arguments, I am of the opinion that...* | *Despite these arguments, I believe that...* |

**Why the simple version is better:**
- It is **clear** – the examiner understands immediately.
- It is **error-free** – no risk of using "delineate" incorrectly.
- It is **natural** – native speakers write this way.
- It saves **time** – you don't struggle to remember rare words.

**The only exception:** Some topics require specific academic vocabulary (e.g., "deforestation", "inflation", "urbanisation"). That is fine. But do not replace "good" with "salubrious" or "bad" with "maleficent".

**Test yourself:** Rewrite this sentence in simple, clear English:

*"It is of paramount importance that the authorities take proactive measures to curtail the proliferation of fake news on social media platforms."*

**Band 9 rewrite:** *"Governments must take action to reduce the spread of fake news on social media."*

If your rewrite looks similar, you are on the right track.

## 5.7 L1 Interference Deep Dive: Articles, Prepositions, Subject-Verb Agreement, Tenses

This section is written specifically for Nigerian speakers of Yoruba, Igbo, and Hausa. These errors are persistent because your native language does not have the same grammatical features.

### 5.7.1 Articles (a/an/the)

**The problem:** Yoruba and Igbo have no articles. Hausa has `da` and `din` but they do not map perfectly to English "a/an/the". As a result, Nigerians frequently omit articles or use them incorrectly.

**The rule simplified:**

| Article | When to use | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **a / an** | For one thing, not specific, mentioned for the first time. Use "an" before vowel sounds (a, e, i, o, u). | *I need **a** new phone.* (any phone) / *She is **an** engineer.* |
| **the** | For a specific thing already mentioned, or unique things (the sun, the government). | *I saw **a** dog. **The** dog was barking.* / * **The** president will speak.* |
| **no article** | For general ideas, plural nouns, uncountable nouns (water, information, education). | *Education is important.* (general) / *Water is essential.* |

**Nigerian drill:** Fill in a/an/the or leave blank (∅):

1. ______ government should build ______ more schools.
2. She wants to become ______ engineer.
3. ______ honesty is ______ best policy.
4. I need ______ information about ______ courses.

**Answers:** 1. The government should build ∅ more schools. (The = specific Nigerian government; ∅ = general schools) 2. an engineer 3. ∅ Honesty is the best policy. 4. ∅ information about the courses. (if specific courses)

**How to train:** For one week, every time you write an English sentence, circle every noun and ask: Is this specific or general? Is it singular or plural? Is it countable or uncountable? This builds automaticity.

### 5.7.2 Prepositions (in, on, at, for, of, to, with, about, etc.)

**The problem:** Nigerian English often uses prepositions differently from standard English. Common examples:

| Wrong (Nigerian) | Right (Standard) |
| :--- | :--- |
| *discuss about* | *discuss* (no preposition) |
| *emphasis on* (correct – but sometimes Nigerians say "emphasis for") | *emphasis on* (actually correct; but the error is using "for" instead of "on") |
| *complain for* | *complain about* |
| *lack of education* (correct) | (no error here, but sometimes "lack for education") |
| *different from* (some Nigerians say "different to" which is British) | *different from* (American) or *different to* (British) – both acceptable |
| *arrive to* | *arrive at* (a place) / *arrive in* (a city/country) |
| *interested on* | *interested in* |
| *depends of* | *depends on* |

**Memory tricks for common Nigerian preposition errors:**
- "Discuss" needs no preposition. Just "discuss the issue". (Imagine you cannot put anything between "discuss" and the object.)
- "Arrive at" for small places (school, hotel). "Arrive in" for large places (Lagos, Nigeria).
- "Interested in" – think "in" as inside your interest zone.
- "Depends on" – think "on" as leaning on something.

**Drill:** Correct the prepositions:

1. We need to discuss about the budget.
2. She arrived to the airport late.
3. I am interested on learning French.
4. The price depends of the quality.
5. He complained for the poor service.

**Answers:** 1. discuss the budget 2. arrived at the airport 3. interested in learning 4. depends on the quality 5. complained about the poor service

### 5.7.3 Subject-Verb Agreement

**The problem:** In Yoruba and Igbo, verbs do not change to agree with the subject. Hausa does have some agreement, but different patterns. So Nigerians often write "He go" instead of "He goes" or "They was" instead of "They were".

**The rule:** Singular subjects (he, she, it, the government, the student) take singular verbs (add -s or -es in present simple). Plural subjects (they, we, the governments, the students) take base verbs.

| Subject | Correct verb | Wrong |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| He / She / It | He works | He work |
| The government (singular) | The government provides | The government provide |
| A student (singular) | A student studies | A student study |
| They / We | They work | They works |
| The governments (plural) | The governments provide | The governments provides |
| Students (plural) | Students study | Students studies |

**Tricky cases for Nigerians:**
- "The number of students **is** increasing." (The number = singular)
- "A number of students **are** late." (A number of = several = plural)
- "Neither the teacher nor the students **are** ready." (The nearest noun 'students' is plural)
- "Each of the students **has** a book." (Each = singular)

**Drill:** Choose the correct verb:

1. The quality of the roads (is / are) poor.
2. Everyone (have / has) the right to education.
3. There (is / are) many solutions to this problem.
4. The committee (was / were) divided in their opinion. (Both possible – committee can be singular or plural depending on whether you see it as one unit or individuals.)

**Answers:** 1. is (quality is singular) 2. has (everyone is singular) 3. are (solutions plural) 4. were (if thinking of individual members; "was" if thinking of the committee as a single body – both acceptable)

### 5.7.4 Tenses

**The problem:** Nigerians often mix past and present in the same sentence or use the wrong tense for time markers.

**Common errors:**
- *"Last year, he **goes** to London."* (should be **went**)
- *"I **have seen** him yesterday."* (should be **saw** – yesterday is specific past, not present perfect)
- *"If I **will see** him, I will tell him."* (should be **see** – first conditional: if + present, will + base)

**Quick tense guide for IELTS Writing:**

| Time context | Tense to use | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| General truths / habits | Present simple | *The government spends money on healthcare.* |
| Past specific time (yesterday, in 2010) | Past simple | *In 2010, the government increased funding.* |
| Past with connection to now (unfinished time: this year, so far) | Present perfect | *The government has increased funding three times since 2010.* |
| Future prediction | Will + base verb | *The government will continue to invest.* |
| Conditional (if...then) | If + present, will + base | *If the government invests more, healthcare will improve.* |

**Drill:** Correct the tense errors:

1. She finish her degree last year.
2. I have never visited Lagos before 2019.
3. If it will rain tomorrow, the event will be cancelled.
4. Since 2000, the population grows rapidly.

**Answers:** 1. finished 2. visited (before 2019 is a past time marker, not present perfect) 3. rains (if it rains) 4. has grown (Since 2000 requires present perfect)

## 5.8 How to Generate Ideas Quickly (30 Seconds per Essay)

Many Nigerians panic because they cannot think of ideas. Here is a **3-step method** that works for any Task 2 prompt.

**Step 1: Identify the topic and the question type** (5 seconds).  
Example prompt: *"Some people believe that social media has a negative impact on mental health. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*  
Topic: social media & mental health. Type: Opinion.

**Step 2: Brainstorm using the "P.E.E.L. table"** (20 seconds). Write down 2–3 quick points.

| Point (your argument) | Explanation (why) | Example (specific) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Social media causes anxiety | Constant comparison with others | Seeing friends' holiday photos |
| Reduces real-life interaction | Less face-to-face conversation | Families at dinner on phones |
| (Counter-argument) Can also connect people | Support groups for mental health | Online therapy communities |

**Step 3: Choose the strongest 2 points for your body paragraphs** (5 seconds). If you agree, use the first two points. If you disagree (or partially agree), include the counter-argument in the third paragraph.

**The secret:** Your examples do not need to be real statistics. You can invent realistic, specific examples. IELTS examiners do not check facts. They check if your example supports your point.

**Bad example (vague):** *"For example, many people feel sad when using social media."*

**Good example (specific and realistic):** *"For example, a teenager who sees classmates posting photos from parties they were not invited to may experience feelings of exclusion and low self-worth."*

**For Nigerian topics:** Use Nigerian examples where possible. It shows relevance and is memorable. *"In Nigeria, the rise of Instagram has led to increased spending on luxury goods as young people try to emulate influencers, often beyond their means."*

## 5.9 Writing Conclusions That Impress

The conclusion is short (2-3 sentences). Do not introduce new ideas. Do not simply repeat your introduction word for word.

**Band 9 conclusion formula:**

| Sentence | Function | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Sentence 1 | Summarise your main points (use different words) | *In conclusion, while social media offers some benefits for connection, its negative effects on mental health – particularly anxiety and reduced social interaction – are significant.* |
| Sentence 2 | Restate your position (can include a recommendation or prediction) | *Therefore, I strongly agree that social media has a negative impact, and I believe that users should set clear boundaries on their usage.* |

**For problem/solution essays:** Summarise the main causes and then state the most important solution.

**Example conclusion (problem/solution):**  
*In conclusion, youth unemployment in Nigeria is driven by a mismatch between education and market needs, as well as a lack of job opportunities. The most effective solution is to reform the educational curriculum to include vocational training and to provide tax incentives for companies that hire young graduates.*

**What not to do:**
- Do not write "In conclusion, this essay has discussed..." (waste of words)
- Do not write "To sum up, I think..." (too weak)
- Do not copy your introduction.

## 5.10 15 Band 9 Model Essays for All 5 Types (Nigerian-Relevant Topics)

The full textbook would contain 15 full essays. Below are **condensed model outlines** for each essay type, plus **one full model essay** for an Opinion type. Use the structure to write your own.

#### Model Essay 1 (Opinion – Full Band 9)

**Prompt:** *"Some people argue that Nigerian universities should focus only on academic subjects, while others believe that practical skills such as entrepreneurship should be included. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*

**Introduction:**  
The debate over whether Nigerian universities should prioritise purely academic learning or incorporate practical skills has become increasingly urgent given the country's high graduate unemployment rate. While academic knowledge remains important, I strongly agree that entrepreneurship and practical skills should be included in university curricula.

**Body Paragraph 1 (Point for including practical skills):**  
Firstly, adding practical skills would directly address graduate unemployment. Many Nigerian graduates leave university with theoretical knowledge but lack the skills to start a business or thrive in a competitive job market. For example, a graduate with training in digital marketing, financial literacy, or small business management could launch a venture immediately after graduation rather than searching for scarce salaried positions. This would reduce the burden on the government and foster economic growth.

**Body Paragraph 2 (Counter-argument and rebuttal):**  
Opponents might argue that universities should protect traditional academic disciplines, which are the foundation of critical thinking. However, practical skills do not replace academics; they complement them. A history student who also learns project management is better equipped for employment without diluting historical knowledge. Furthermore, countries like Germany have successfully integrated vocational training into higher education without sacrificing academic rigour.

**Conclusion:**  
In conclusion, while academic subjects remain essential, Nigerian universities must urgently incorporate practical skills such as entrepreneurship. This dual approach would reduce unemployment, stimulate innovation, and produce graduates who are both thoughtful and employable. Therefore, I fully support curriculum reform.

## 5.13 The 10-Minute Planning Method (For 40-Minute Essays)

You have 40 minutes for Task 2. Spend the first 10 minutes **only planning**. Do not start writing until you have a clear plan.

**The 10-minute breakdown:**

```
- Example: _________________

- Example: _________________

- Example: _________________

Conclusion summary: _________________
```

With a solid plan, writing becomes fast and organised. Without a plan, you will write in circles and likely miss the question.

# UNIT 6: SPEAKING LIKE AN EXPERT

## 6.1 The IELTS Speaking Test – What Nigerians Need to Know

The Speaking test is 11–14 minutes, face-to-face with an examiner (often a non-Nigerian). It has 3 parts.

| Part | Duration | What happens | What examiners assess |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Part 1** | 4–5 minutes | Personal questions (home, family, work, hobbies, daily routines) | Fluency, pronunciation, ability to expand answers naturally |
| **Part 2** | 3–4 minutes | You get a cue card, have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1–2 minutes | Extended speaking, organisation, vocabulary |
| **Part 3** | 4–5 minutes | Abstract questions related to Part 2 topic (e.g., "Why do some people prefer cities?") | Ability to discuss abstract ideas, justify opinions, use complex grammar |

**Why Nigerians lose points in Speaking:**

| Problem | How it affects you |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Short answers in Part 1** | "Do you like your job?" – "Yes." (No expansion = low fluency score) |
| **Pronunciation of 'th' and vowel length** | Examiner misunderstands you or finds it tiring to listen to. |
| **Memorised answers** | Examiner spots your memorised speech and caps your score at 6.0. |
| **Not using the 1-minute prep in Part 2** | You start speaking without structure and run out of ideas. |
| **Part 3 answers too short or too personal** | Part 3 requires general, abstract discussion, not personal stories. |

**The Band 9 mindset:** Speak naturally, like you are talking to a friend who is interested. Do not try to sound like a professor. Do not memorise. Do not rush.

---

## 6.2 Part 1 – From "Fine" to Fluent (Expanding Answers Naturally)

The most common Nigerian mistake in Part 1 is giving one-word or very short answers.

**Example (bad):**
- Examiner: *Do you enjoy reading books?*
- Candidate: *Yes.* (pause) *I like it.*

**Example (Band 9):**
- Examiner: *Do you enjoy reading books?*
- Candidate: *Yes, definitely. I've been an avid reader since I was a child. I particularly enjoy fiction, especially Nigerian authors like Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie. It's a great way to relax after work.*

**The formula for expansion (use the "ABC" method):**

| Letter | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **A** | Answer the question directly | *Yes, definitely.* |
| **B** | Because (give a reason) | *I've been an avid reader since I was a child.* |
| **C** | Concrete detail (specific example) | *I particularly enjoy Nigerian authors like Adichie.* |
| **D** | (Optional) Further comment | *It's a great way to relax after work.* |

**Practice:** For any Part 1 question (common topics: work/study, home, hobbies, weather, technology, friends, food), train yourself to give A+B+C in 15–20 seconds.

**Common Part 1 questions for Nigerians (prepare answers):**

| Question | Your A+B+C |
| :--- | :--- |
| *Do you work or study?* | |
| *What do you like about your hometown?* | |
| *How often do you use social media?* | |
| *What kind of food do you enjoy?* | |

Write your answers in your notebook. Practice saying them aloud until they sound natural (not memorised).

**Pronunciation tip for Part 1:** The examiner hears your pronunciation immediately. Focus on clear 'th' sounds and correct vowel length. For example, do not say "tree" for "three" or "sheet" for "shit" (embarrassing mistake). Practice minimal pairs daily.

---

## 6.3 Part 2 – The 1-Minute Preparation Strategy

You will get a cue card like this:

> **Describe a gift you gave to someone.**
> You should say:
> - Who you gave it to
> - What the gift was
> - Why you chose that gift
> - And explain how the person reacted.

You have **1 minute to prepare**. Use this time to make notes – do not write full sentences. The examiner will give you a paper and pencil.

**The Band 9 note-taking method (1 minute):**

| Seconds | Action | Example notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0–10 | Read the 4 bullet points. Underline keywords. | gift / who / what / why / reaction |
| 10–30 | Think of a specific real or realistic example. Write key words (not sentences). | *Who: my mother / What: watch / Why: her old one broken / Reaction: cried happy* |
| 30–50 | Think of 2–3 descriptive details or a small story. | *surprised / opened slowly / hugged me* |
| 50–60 | Decide your opening sentence. | *I'd like to talk about...* |

**Your 1-minute notes (example):**

```
Mom – 50th birthday
Watch – silver, engraved
Her old watch stopped – she missed it
Reaction: tears, wears every day
```

**Then speak for 1–2 minutes.** Do not try to cover all 4 bullet points perfectly; use them as a structure. Aim for 90–120 seconds.

**What to do if you run out of ideas:**
- Add a small story: *"When I gave it to her, she didn't open it immediately. She saved it for after dinner..."*
- Describe feelings: *"I felt nervous because I wasn't sure if she would like it..."*
- Compare: *"Unlike her old watch, this one has a leather strap..."*

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not memorise an answer. The examiner will notice your eyes looking up/away.
- Do not speak for less than 1 minute. Practice with a timer.
- Do not pause for more than 3 seconds. If you pause, use a filler: *"Let me think...", "That's a good question..."*

## 6.4 Pronunciation Focus for Nigerians: 'th', Vowel Length, Word Stress

This section could alone raise your Speaking score by 1 full band.

### 6.4.1 The 'th' Sounds (voiced and unvoiced)

Nigerians often pronounce 'th' as 't' or 'd'. Example: "three" becomes "tree", "that" becomes "dat".

**Two 'th' sounds in English:**

| Sound | Symbol | How to make it | Example words |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Unvoiced (no vibration in throat) | /θ/ | Tongue between teeth, blow air | think, three, thanks, both, month |
| Voiced (vibration in throat) | /ð/ | Tongue between teeth, hum | this, that, these, those, mother, father |

**Practice drill (say aloud slowly):**

Unvoiced: *think, third, birthday, nothing, both, teeth*
Voiced: *this, that, these, those, weather, mother, breathe*

**Minimal pairs (practice daily):**

| /θ/ (unvoiced) | /ð/ (voiced) |
| :--- | :--- |
| thigh | thy (archaic, but practice) |
| teeth | teethe (verb) |
| ether | either |
| mouth (noun) | mouth (verb) |

**Nigerian trick:** If you cannot make the 'th' sound, try smiling slightly. This lifts your tongue to the right position. Do not substitute 't' or 'd'.

### 6.4.2 Vowel Length (Short vs Long Vowels)

Many Nigerian languages do not distinguish between short and long vowels. In English, changing vowel length changes meaning.

| Short vowel word | Long vowel word | Difference in meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| ship (short i) | sheep (long ee) | a boat vs an animal |
| live (short i) | leave (long ee) | to reside vs to depart |
| full (short u) | fool (long oo) | not empty vs a stupid person |
| beach (long ee) | bitch (short i) | sand vs insult |

**Practice drill (say each pair, stretching the long vowel):**

*ship – sheep / live – leave / sit – seat / bit – beat / full – fool / pull – pool / beach – bitch*

**Nigerian tip:** For long vowels, imagine the sound echoing. For short vowels, cut the sound off sharply.

### 6.4.3 Word Stress (Which Syllable is Stronger?)

Nigerians often stress the wrong syllable, making words hard to understand.

**Rule:** In most two-syllable nouns and adjectives, stress the **first** syllable. In most two-syllable verbs, stress the **second** syllable.

| Word type | Example | Correct stress |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Noun | *record* (the object) | RE-cord |
| Verb | *record* (the action) | re-CORD |
| Noun | *present* (a gift) | PRE-sent |
| Verb | *present* (to show) | pre-SENT |

**Common Nigerian stress errors:**

| Word | Wrong stress (Nigerian) | Right stress |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| *hotel* | HO-tel | ho-TEL |
| *police* | PO-lice | po-LICE |
| *magazine* | MA-ga-zine | ma-ga-ZINE |
| *develop* | DE-vel-op | de-VEL-op |

**How to improve:** Use any online dictionary (e.g., Cambridge Dictionary) that has a speaker icon. Listen to the stress pattern. Repeat 10 times.

## 6.5 Idioms & Collocations – When to Use (And Not Overuse)

Examiners want to see **natural** use of idiomatic language. Do not force idioms into every sentence.

**Good (natural):**
- *"I was on the fence about which university to choose."* (undecided)
- *"It's a win-win situation for both students and employers."* (both benefit)

**Bad (forced or wrong):**
- *"I think it's a piece of cake to learn English."* (overused cliché)
- *"The government should kill two birds with one stone."* (may sound odd in formal writing – but in speaking, it's acceptable if natural)

**Better than idioms: use collocations.** Collocations are words that naturally go together. They impress examiners more than idioms.

| Weak (Band 6) | Strong collocation (Band 9) |
| :--- | :--- |
| *very important* | *crucial / essential / of paramount importance* |
| *make a decision* | *reach a decision / arrive at a decision* |
| *big problem* | *major issue / significant challenge* |
| *get a job* | *secure employment / land a position* |

**For Band 9 Speaking:** Use 2–3 natural collocations per minute, zero or one idiom. That is enough.

## 6.6 Part 3 – Abstract Thinking and Extended Answers

Part 3 is the most challenging because the questions are **abstract** (not about you personally). The examiner will ask things like: *"Why do some people prefer living in cities?"* or *"What are the effects of social media on democracy?"*

**Key difference from Part 1:**
- Part 1: personal, concrete, short answers (A+B+C)
- Part 3: general, abstract, longer answers (4–6 sentences)

**The Band 9 method for Part 3 (the "4-Sentence Rule"):**

| Sentence | Function | Example (question: *Why do people migrate from rural to urban areas?*) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Sentence 1 | Direct answer + general reason | *In my view, the main reason people move to cities is to seek better economic opportunities.* |
| Sentence 2 | Explain or elaborate | *Rural areas often lack sufficient jobs, and wages tend to be lower. Cities, by contrast, offer more diverse employment and higher salaries.* |
| Sentence 3 | Give a specific example or evidence | *For instance, in Nigeria, many young people from villages move to Lagos because they hope to find work in tech, retail, or services – sectors that are hardly present in rural regions.* |
| Sentence 4 | Conclude or add a contrasting point (optional) | *Of course, some also move for education or healthcare, but economic factors are usually the primary driver.* |

**Total:** 4 sentences, about 45–60 seconds. Perfect for Part 3.

**Practice:** For each of these common Part 3 topics, write a 4-sentence answer:

1. *What are the advantages of learning a foreign language?*
2. *Why do some people prefer online shopping to physical stores?*
3. *How does unemployment affect society?*
4. *What can governments do to reduce pollution?*

**Nigerian tip:** In Part 3, avoid giving personal examples ("my cousin moved to Lagos..."). Instead, give general examples ("many young Nigerians..."). Part 3 is about society, not you.

**If you don't know the answer:** It's fine to say *"That's an interesting question. I haven't thought about it much, but I suppose..."* This buys you time and sounds natural.

## 6.7 50 Speaking Cue Cards with Band 9 Sample Answers (Condensed)

The full textbook would contain 50 cue cards. Below are **5 representative cue cards** with full Band 9 sample answers. Use the same structure for the remaining 45 (topics: people, places, objects, events, activities, etc.).

#### Cue Card 1 (Person)

**Describe a person who has influenced you.**
You should say:
- Who this person is
- How you know them
- What qualities they have
- And explain why they influenced you.

**Band 9 sample answer (1 minute 45 seconds):**

*"I'd like to talk about my secondary school English teacher, Mrs. Adeyemi. I knew her for three years, from ages 14 to 17, because she taught me in both JSS 3 and SS 1. She had several remarkable qualities. First, she was incredibly patient – she would explain grammar points multiple times without ever getting frustrated. Second, she was passionate about literature; she made Shakespeare and Achebe come alive in the classroom. To this day, I still remember her saying, 'A good question is better than a good answer.' That changed how I learn."*

**Why this is Band 9:**
- Specific details (names, ages, quotes)
- Natural storytelling (before/after contrast)
- Emotional connection (remembered quote)
- No memorised phrases; sounds authentic.

#### Cue Card 2 (Place)

**Describe a quiet place you like to visit.**
You should say:
- Where it is
- How often you go there
- What you do there
- And explain why you like it.

**Band 9 sample answer:**

*"A place I really treasure is the botanical garden in my city, near the university. It's about a 20-minute walk from my house. I try to go there at least once a week, usually on Saturday mornings when it's not crowded. When I'm there, I either read a book on one of the benches or just walk slowly and look at the plants. #### Cue Card 3 (Object)

**Describe a gift you received that was memorable.**

- What the gift was
- Who gave it to you
- When you received it
- And explain why it was memorable.

**Band 9 sample answer:**

*"The most memorable gift I ever received was a wristwatch from my father on my 18th birthday. It wasn't an expensive brand – just a simple silver Casio – but the engraving on the back said, 'To my son, time is the only thing you can't buy back.' Whenever I'm tempted to procrastinate, I look at the watch and remember his words. It's not just a gift; it's a piece of advice I carry with me."*

#### Cue Card 4 (Event)

**Describe a celebration you attended recently.**
You should say:
- What the celebration was
- Where it took place
- Who was there
- And explain why it was enjoyable.

**Band 9 sample answer:**

*"Last month, I attended my cousin's wedding in Enugu. It was a traditional Igbo wedding, so the colours were very vibrant – everyone wore gele and agbada. The ceremony took place at my uncle's compound, which had been decorated with palm fronds and colourful fabrics. There must have been over 200 guests, including family from all over the country and even some from abroad. What made it enjoyable was the sense of community. At a Nigerian wedding, it's not just about the couple; it's about everyone coming together to eat, dance, and celebrate. The highlight was when the groom's family brought out the wine for the bride to find – everyone was cheering and laughing. Also, the food was amazing – jollof rice, fried plantains, and goat meat pepper soup. I danced until my feet hurt. It reminded me how important family and tradition are in our culture."*

- How often you do it
- Where you do it
- And explain why it helps you stay healthy.

## 6.9 Common Nigerian Errors in Speaking (With Corrections)

| Error type | Wrong (Nigerian common) | Right (Band 9) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pronouncing 'th' as 't' or 'd'** | *I tink it's good.* | *I think it's good.* |
| **Short vowel instead of long** | *I live in Lagos* (but meant *leave*) | *I live* (reside) vs *I leave* (depart) – context helps, but still wrong |
| **Wrong word stress** | *The ho-tel is nice.* | *The ho-TEL is nice.* |
| **Missing 's' for third person singular** | *He work hard.* | *He works hard.* |
| **Using 'been' as past of 'go'** | *I have been to market and I am still there.* (confusing) | *I have gone to market* (not back) or *I have been to market* (back) |
| **Asking a question without inversion** | *What you are doing?* | *What are you doing?* |
| **Using 'no' instead of 'not'** | *It is no good.* | *It is not good.* |
| **Overusing 'very'** | *It is very very good.* | *It is excellent / wonderful.* |
| **Saying 'I am having' for possession** | *I am having a car.* | *I have a car.* |
| **Responding with 'OK' to everything** | *OK.* (instead of expanding) | *That's a good question... / Let me think...* |

**How to fix in speaking:** Record yourself answering 5 Part 1 questions. Transcribe exactly what you said (write it down). Circle every error using the table above. Then re-record the same answers correctly. Repeat until errors disappear.

# UNIT 8: TEST-DAY STRATEGY

## 8.1 One Week Before: What to Do and Avoid

**Do:**
- Confirm your test location, time, and required documents (passport, printout).
- Plan your route to the test centre. Do a trial run if possible.
- Prepare your bag: passport (same as registered), water bottle (clear), pen/pencil, eraser, sharpener.
- Sleep 8 hours every night.
- Eat balanced meals (protein, complex carbs, not just sugar).

**Avoid:**
- Learning new vocabulary (too late – focus on review).
- Taking a full mock test (will exhaust you).
- Staying up late to study.
- Discussing the test with anxious friends.
- Drinking too much caffeine (makes you jittery).

---

## 8.2 The Night Before & Morning Of

**Night before:**
- Pack your bag. Put it by the door.
- Set two alarms (phone + physical alarm).
- Lay out your clothes (comfortable layers – test centre may be cold).
- Eat dinner at least 2 hours before bed.
- Stop all screens 1 hour before bed. Read a light book or listen to calm music.

**Morning of the test:**
- Wake up early enough to eat breakfast (porridge, bread, fruit – nothing heavy or greasy).
- Drink water, but not too much (limited toilet breaks during the test).
- Arrive at the test centre **1 hour early** – traffic can be unpredictable.
- Breathe deeply for 2 minutes before entering. Tell yourself: *"I have prepared. I will do my best."*

---

## 8.3 Managing Anxiety (Especially for Speaking)

Speaking anxiety is common among Nigerians, especially when the examiner is not Nigerian.

**Techniques to calm nerves:**

| Technique | How to do it |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Box breathing** | Inhale 4 seconds, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat 5 times before entering the room. |
| **Power pose** | Stand with hands on hips, chest open, for 2 minutes. Reduces cortisol (stress hormone). |
| **Reframe the examiner** | Imagine the examiner is a friendly colleague or a curious uncle who wants to hear your story. Not a judge. |
| **First sentence prepared** | For Part 1, have a standard opener: *"Good morning. My name is [X]. I'm ready."* Speaking the first words breaks the ice. |

**During the Speaking test, if you make a mistake:**
- Do not apologise excessively. Say *"Sorry, let me rephrase that"* and continue.
- Do not stop speaking. Keep going. Fluency is more important than perfection.
- If you forget a word, describe it: *"It's the thing you use to... ah, a phone charger."* That shows communication skill.

---

## 8.4 What to Do If You Make a Mistake During the Test

**Listening:**
- Missed an answer? **Move on immediately.** Do not panic. One wrong is allowed for Band 9.
- Cannot hear a word? Guess. Write something.

**Reading:**
- Running out of time? Guess all remaining answers (choose 'True' for T/F/NG, or 'A' for multiple choice). Never leave blanks.
- Cannot find the answer after 1 minute? Guess and put a "?" mark. Return if time.

**Writing:**
- Halfway through and realise you misunderstood the question? Do not delete everything. Adapt. Add a sentence: *"Although the question focuses on X, it is also relevant to consider Y..."* This is better than panic.
- Running out of time for Task 2? Write a quick conclusion even if your body paragraphs are short. An unfinished essay scores very low.

**Speaking:**
- Paused for too long? Use a filler: *"Let me think about that for a moment... Well, I suppose..."*
- Realised you made a grammar error? Do not stop. Just correct yourself and continue: *"He go to school every day – sorry, he goes to school every day."*
